Proposal

Short Summary

We will create a prototype for a mobile application and widget that provides information about travel options that are proximal to the user. Our most immediate goal is to have this work within the Portland metro area. This will better enable users to leverage sustainable transportation options.

We believe we can impact this goal with our project by increasing productivity and encouraging greater use of sustainable transportation options. This can be achieved by increasing the efficiency of existing technologies and transportation options. Users can be better enabled to utilize efficient transportation and feel empowered to do so.

Ideal concept would be a widget that quickly connects a user to their destination in the quickest, least expensive and most environmentally sustainable manner. This could be connecting a series of transportation types and options which could chain together to maximize efficiency and minimize impact.

Observing Users

At various times, multiple people were observed using TriMet MAX. They generally used the mobile wallet tap-to-pay functionality to board and experienced long wait times between trains. Passengers carried a variety of items, including backpacks, purses, personal transportation devices, and even overnight luggage. Many individuals brought bikes, strollers, or wheelchairs and other assistive devices, facilitating smooth transitions between MAX and bus services. Similarly, TriMet buses saw numerous riders managing a range of items akin to those seen on MAX.

Additionally, bicycles were a common mode of commuting, alongside cars, which offered freedom and flexibility but could be costly and inefficient unless carpooling. Ride-sharing services like Uber and Lyft also appeared as commuting options, though they shared similar drawbacks in terms of expense and efficiency without carpooling.

We also observed alternative forms of transportation, highlighting a strong association with safety and weather considerations. This included manual and e-bikes, manual and e-scooters, and various one-wheeled electric devices—but it also included heavier forms like motorcycles and mopeds.

Identifying the Problem

In order for people to feel empowered to leverage sustainable transportation options, it must be efficient and effective. Unfortunately, some of the more sustainable transportation options are not efficient. This disproportionately impacts the poor who are most likely to be negatively impacted by delays related to public transportation. They are also least likely to be able to afford the most advanced sustainable transportation options. The current state is a frustrating maze of varying applications and pay mechanisms. Some transportation options may also not be immediately obvious or available to all individuals.

Finding Related Products

The main two related products we explored were the Transit app and Google Maps.

The Transit app provides live transit data and encourages its users to provide information about their bus or train ride by “gamifying” the experience and awarding cooperative users with points that can place them on a monthly leaderboard. It also provides bicycling data, but it will only suggest bicycling if no transit routes are available and does not take weather into account.

Google Maps prioritizes driving over all other methods of transportation. Its bicycling data also does not take weather into account and its transit data is not as user-friendly as the Transit app. Live bus locations and headways are hidden in a submenu in both the app and website.

Both of these products also default to requiring a route be actively input by the user, rather than using their commute at all times. Apple Maps, for instance, suggests commuting options over entering a route when location data indicates you are at home or at work. It suffers from the same limitations as Google Maps, however.

Comparing Products

Our solution will encourage users to use active or public transportation over private cars and ride-sharing. It will combine the useful bicycle data of the Transit app with weather data to suggest taking public transportation on a rainy day or biking on a sunny day. It will also be a specifically commute-focused app which always has directions and the most optimal commuting option available.

Designing at a High-Level Architecture of the Proposed System

High-level Architecture Diagram (Show/Hide)

The system will consist of a mobile app and a home screen widget for mobile devices. The mobile app will be used to configure a user’s commute, while the best commuting option will remain displayed to the user in the widget.

Justifying Feasibility

Weeks 1–2: We will begin by establishing the key features of our project and drafting the initial design for the website and its connected features.

Weeks 3–6: This phase will involve constructing and coding the project, applying an iterative process to develop the basic functions, architecture, and artifacts. This will likely be the most intensive part of our sprint, requiring close collaboration to refine key concepts and assess what features can realistically be achieved within the semester. During this time, we will also assemble surveys and start aggregating responses. We may utilize Python and the OpenAI API to generate concise directions for navigation, as outlined in our ideas addendum. Ari, with experience in iOS and Android app development as well as website creation using GitHub Pages, will lead development efforts, while Travis will focus on enhancing his core programming skills. He brings experience in R, Python, and SQL, which will allow him to contribute to coding tasks and assist in generating visuals using AI-assisted image tools. Additionally, we plan to leverage ChatGPT for coding assistance to accelerate our progress and simplify the learning of essential components.

Weeks 7–8: In this stage, we will test and iterate on our initial versions, ensuring that they closely resemble the final presentation. We will confirm the structure for our class demonstration and identify any viable features to add, while removing unnecessary ones. We will also create and organize survey feedback, evaluating and analyzing the responses to inform our updates.

Weeks 9–10: The final weeks will be dedicated to finalizing key components of the project in preparation for our presentation. We will incorporate and address the feedback received throughout the process and outline future revisions and next steps, establishing a vision for the project's future.

Throughout all phases of the project, we will document our code, ensure that we continuously iterate to ensure our product and strategy are in line with the wants and needs of our users, and we will remain open to removing or reforming features which may not work for the small scale of this course.
